Unfinished House

Unfinished House

M.Arch / Studio 1

Rhino - Revit - Photoshop - Physical

This house showcases a gradient between the finished and the unfinished, predictable and unpredictable, represented through a burying of one half of the building, encased and protected, that is then fractured by a public path through the center. This facilitates a release and extension of the rest of the house as it expands to the left, while at the same time creating an interface with the public. Through this interface the shadows cast by individuals as they walk across the path and the moments created as people sit and lounge upon the site work takes the unpredictable and injects it into the space; thus the architecture of the unfinished becomes complete.

The design of the unfinished house evolved out of objective and subjective studies of a given site within Calgary, as well as through a framework developed by studying a given artist -- in the case of this project, Francis Bacon.

The essence of the unfinished comes across most in section for this project; the buried, embedded, “finished” portion on the right hand side is fractured by a public path driven through the middle of the house. From there the house dissolves towards the left, becoming open, unfinished, and inviting public interaction to complete the architecture.

Sprout

Sprout

M.Arch / Studio 2

Rhino - Blender - Illustrator - Physical

Sprout serves as a fertile ground for new connections and community growth -- a place for strangers, neighbours, and visitors from far away to come together and foster new bonds in a welcoming space.

This project is a unique combination of a live-work hostel that provides free accommodation in return for working 25 hours per week in Sprout’s secondary program: an ever evolving community kitchen featuring a rotating menu of family recipes and traditional dishes shared by those who visit this hostel.

This core concept of connection, collaboration, and sharing is furthered by overall layout and form of Sprout; eschewing corridors and traditional circulation space, instead focusing all attention inward on a large shared atrium space housing the kitchen, lounge, lobby, and many more spaces within.

Sprout features a gradient of introversion and extroversion, of public and private. The ground floor features the main entrance, kitchen, and bar, and is thus the most extroverted and public zone. The amount of privacy increases as one travels upwards, bringing in spaces of reflection, work share, and even onwards into private bedrooms within the third floor.

SPMC

Sun Peaks Medical Center

Undergraduate Design Studio / ARET

Revit - Lumion - Physical

Taking cues from the Sun Peaks ski-hill itself, as well as sleek and modern ski helmets, this medical center was designed to be bright, open, and visually striking from any distance, yet not out of place at the foot of the snowy slopes. This idea of openness was taken another step by the addition of a ski-through corridor through the middle of the medical center, improving both access to the medical center as well as reducing congestion in and around the building. The roof slopes alternate so as to maximize southern exposure and capitalize on the winter sun that the ski-hill and surrounding municipality are named for.

Not just a medical center either, the upper floors also included mixed programming such as conference space, a library, and a cafe -- each with large windows in order to enjoy the striking views of the nearby slopes.

Bones

Bones

M.Arch / Studio 2

Rhino - Photoshop - Physical

“In a world built on money, the bones represent the decaying husk of the beast - the cycle of commerce and capital that all of us are a part of.”

This project began as an exploration of site at both the macro and micro scale -- with more of a focus on the latter as we photographed and manipulated textures and materials found throughout the area. Then, using a chosen reading on the role architects must play in commerce as well as society as a framework, the bones were designed as an object intervention into the site, responding to both the reading as well as the discoveries, textures, and materials made during the site exploration.

The Bones grew out of these studied patterns and materials from the site, and are arranged to create a linear structure mimicking a ribcage with small spaces, pods, to share and temporarily claim as your own. From the outside of the Bones, any pod that is claimed showcases this fact through glowing strip lighting running along the outside of the spot.

Precedent

Precedent Study

M.Arch / Graphics 2

Rhino - Blender - Photoshop - Illustrator

As a part of a multi-family residential precedent study, I examined and fully remodelled this famous apartment tower, The Ardmore Residence, located in Singapore, designed by UNStudio and built in 2013.

The 3D geometry of the tower was built entirely with Rhino7, and from there it was taken into other programs such as photoshop or Blender in order to create various renderings or drawings of the tower.

Form Finding

Form Finding

M.Arch / Graphics 1

Rhino - Photoshop - Physical - Illustrator

Over the course of the entire semester, a final form was slowly worked towards though a variety of graphical explorations. Beginning with simply recreating precedent house plans, and then creating “Architectural Jazz” with them, a variety of 3D forms were found.

From there, further explorations in generating the interior and determining the final massing and material of the object, or form, were conducted. The model on the left is the final result of this process.

Using a kit of parts derived from a variety of different precedent floor plans, new forms, layouts, or 'profiles' were discovered and generated via the iterative process of “Architectural Jazz”. These profiles were then used as the basis to again iteratively generate 3D objects, or characters, in large numbers. One of these characters would be chosen as the final form upon which explorations of material would be done.

This chosen form was explored in plan, section, and elevation. The interior was added in a way so as to not have it perfectly equal the exterior. Multiple explorations of material and texture were done through photoshop, with one chosen iteration being manufactured and hand built to finalize the form.

Cathedris is an ongoing creative outlet -- it's a worldbuilding project for the sake of creation. It's created online, piece by piece, article by article, on the platform known as World Anvil. These articles detail the worlds history, its gods, species, countries and more. Many of the articles were created as part of platform-wide challenges or competitions, during which the combination of my writing and art has won many awards and accolades.

This world is dark and foreboding, playing with themes of hope versus despair within an industrial world. The world of Cathedris was created with the guidance of colossal gods that helped form it -- but Cathedris as a worldbuilding project begins when those same gods all "died". However, being gods, their bodies were too powerful to properly die. What remained were the "God-husks", giant mindless beings cursed to wander the planet for eternity.

This event that created the God-husks took place many thousands of years ago, and was the origin point of modern day Cathedris. Civilizations grew and matured under the shadow of these dead gods, and as a result, much of society is based around the way they impact the world within which they wander.
